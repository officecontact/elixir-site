Healthcare AI: Building HIPAA-Compliant Solutions That Actually Work

A technical guide to deploying AI in healthcare environments — covering data governance, model validation, and FDA SaMD requirements

Why Healthcare AI is Different

Building AI for healthcare isn't just technically harder — it's governed by a regulatory framework that has no equivalent in other industries. HIPAA in the US, GDPR and NHS Data Security standards in the UK, FDA Software as a Medical Device (SaMD) guidance, and clinical governance requirements all shape what you can build, how you can build it, and what evidence you need before deployment.

This post is a practical guide for engineering and clinical teams navigating this environment.

PHI Handling: The Non-Negotiable Foundation

Protected Health Information (PHI) includes more than you might expect: not just names and medical record numbers, but dates of service, geographic information below state level, phone numbers, IP addresses, and any other data that could be used to identify a patient.

In practice, a healthcare AI system that handles PHI must:

  • Encrypt PHI at rest (AES-256 minimum) and in transit (TLS 1.2+)
  • Implement strict access controls with role-based permissions and audit logging
  • Have a signed Business Associate Agreement (BAA) with every vendor who handles PHI — including your LLM provider
  • Have a documented Data Processing Agreement covering breach notification timelines

For LLM-based applications, this means you cannot send PHI to third-party API endpoints without a BAA. OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google Cloud all offer HIPAA-eligible services with BAAs — but you must explicitly enable this configuration and understand what data logging and retention policies apply.

The De-identification Option

Many healthcare AI use cases don't actually require identified data. If your model is learning patterns — predicting readmission risk, classifying scan results, identifying documentation gaps — it can often be trained on de-identified data and applied to identified data at inference time (with appropriate access controls).

HIPAA provides two safe harbour standards for de-identification:

  • Expert determination: A qualified expert applies statistical methods to verify residual re-identification risk is very small
  • Safe harbour: Remove 18 specific categories of identifiers and have no actual knowledge that residual information could identify the patient

De-identification significantly simplifies your data governance obligations and opens up data sharing possibilities (training on multi-site data, using research data for commercial models) that would otherwise require complex data use agreements.

FDA SaMD: When Does Your AI Become a Medical Device?

In the US, software that meets the definition of a medical device is regulated by the FDA. The definition is broad: software that is "intended to be used for diagnosis, cure, mitigation, treatment, or prevention of disease" meets the threshold.

Key questions to ask:

  • Does the AI output inform a clinical decision? (Yes = probably SaMD)
  • Is the AI intended to replace clinical judgement, or augment it? (Replace = higher risk class)
  • What happens if the AI is wrong? (Higher severity = higher regulatory bar)

FDA has a risk-based classification framework. Class I (low risk, general controls) through Class III (high risk, premarket approval). Most clinical decision support AI falls in Class II, requiring 510(k) clearance.

The UK has equivalent requirements under MHRA's Software as a Medical Device guidance, which is broadly aligned with the FDA framework but has some UK-specific elements post-Brexit.

Model Validation in Clinical Context

Healthcare AI validation goes beyond standard ML validation metrics. You need:

  • Clinical validation: Performance evaluated on a clinically representative population, not just a random test split. Age, sex, ethnicity, and comorbidity distributions must match your deployment population.
  • Subgroup analysis: Model performance must be disaggregated across patient subgroups. A model with 95% accuracy that performs at 80% for a particular ethnic group is unacceptable in clinical practice.
  • Prospective validation: Where possible, validate prospectively (real patients, real workflow) not just retrospectively on historical data.
  • Clinician usability testing: The system must be evaluated with actual clinicians in realistic workflows. Technical performance doesn't predict clinical utility.

Practical Deployment Checklist

Before deploying any clinical AI system:

  1. BAAs in place with all vendors handling PHI
  2. Data use agreement covering all data sources
  3. Clinical governance approval from relevant committee
  4. IT security assessment completed
  5. FDA/MHRA regulatory pathway assessed and (if required) cleared
  6. Clinical validation study documented and peer-reviewed
  7. Clinician training and change management plan in place
  8. Post-deployment monitoring plan with defined performance thresholds
  9. Incident response plan for model failures
  10. Model update/retraining governance process defined
